The Big Shuffle

Mapping the migrant detention flight network
Visualization by Jon Nealon

In 2025, ICE Air operated over 8,700 domestic shuffle flights — internal transfers between U.S. detention facilities serving no deportation purpose. Flown by a small group of private contractors billing by the hour, these flights generated millions in revenue while moving thousands of migrants across the country. This map traces that network.

Under the Trump administration's renewed immigration crackdown, ICE detention capacity expanded rapidly. Arrests surged, facilities filled beyond capacity, and the contracting apparatus that moves detainees between them grew with it — generating millions in revenue for a small group of private aviation companies operating largely outside public view.

The Network

The shuffle network runs not through major commercial hubs but through small regional airports — many of them military fields or rural facilities with minimal public oversight. Alexandria, Louisiana and Harlingen, Texas emerge as the dominant nodes, processing thousands of transfers annually. Each circle represents an active shuffle airport, scaled by total flight volume.

The Acceleration

Through 2025, the pace of internal shuffle flights accelerated sharply. In January — the final month of the Biden administration — fewer than 300 shuffle flights were recorded. By December, that number had grown to nearly 1,200 per month, a fourfold increase tracking the expansion of ICE enforcement operations.
